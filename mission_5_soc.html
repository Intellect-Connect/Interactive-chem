The inventory is now as safe as it can be. The water quality report is filed. But 5,000 residents still need to eat — and not every household has a stove, a full kitchen, or the time and budget that most recipes assume. What good is a safe food supply if no one knows how to cook with it?

This is a different kind of chemistry problem. You are not detecting a contaminant. You are designing something — a recipe that works within real constraints: no stove, $1.50 per serving maximum, and ingredients from the verified-safe inventory. The chemistry you have built all semester — polarity, solubility, mixture behavior, molecular interactions — governs exactly how these recipes function, and temperature is the variable you have not yet controlled for.
Your assignment: Design a nutritious, budget-compliant recipe using only verified-safe inventory items. Choose a temperature tier (cold, room temperature, or hot water only), explain the chemistry of how your recipe works at that temperature, calculate the nutrition and cost per serving, and contribute your recipe to the Bracket City Community Cookbook.
Real-World Context — Why This Matters

Food insecurity is not only about access to food — it is about access to usable food. A household without a stove, or a family living in a motel or shelter, may have access to canned beans and rolled oats but no reliable way to prepare them. In 2023, approximately 47 million Americans lived in food-insecure households, a figure that includes millions who rely on food banks, pantries, and community kitchens where cooking facilities are limited or shared. [Feeding America — Map the Meal Gap]

Community cookbooks have a long history as tools for knowledge-sharing in exactly these contexts. When a food bank publishes a recipe collection designed around its own inventory — with realistic equipment assumptions and verified nutritional value — it becomes a practical resource that extends the impact of every donated item. The chemistry matters here in a direct way: a recipe that fails to account for how starch gelatinizes, how emulsions behave at room temperature, or how protein solubility changes with heat will produce food that is unpalatable or nutritionally incomplete, regardless of how good the individual ingredients are. [USDA — Food Insecurity in America]

Temperature changes what food can do. Pour boiling water over oats and they soften into a creamy porridge within minutes. Pour cold water over the same oats and leave them overnight — you still get something edible, but it works completely differently. The hot version works because heat gives water molecules enough energy to force their way into the starch granules and break them open, which releases the thickening effect. The cold version works because water slowly soaks in over hours. Same ingredients. Same result, roughly. Completely different chemistry getting you there — and if you mix up which method applies to which ingredient, your recipe either fails to thicken or turns into paste. [ScienceDirect — Starch Gelatinization]

This is where your semester comes together. You cannot design a recipe that works without understanding why peanut butter separates at room temperature (nonpolar fats versus polar proteins, no emulsifier to hold them together), why salt dissolves invisibly into water while oat flour clouds it (ionic dissolution versus suspension), or why a mug soup thickens with hot water but stays thin when cold (gelatinization threshold). Every constraint in this lab — no stove, $1.50, safe inventory — is a design problem that chemistry helps you solve.

Temperature and Solubility — The Connection in Food
3 pts

Water (H₂O) is a polar molecule — it dissolves other polar substances like salt (NaCl), sugar (C₁₂H₂₂O₁₁), and honey readily, while nonpolar substances like oils and fats do not mix with it. But polarity is only half the story. Temperature changes how much of a substance can dissolve — and how fast. The chart below shows this relationship for four food bank ingredients. Types of Mixtures in Food
3 pts
Solution homogeneous mixture
Solute disappears evenly into solvent — clear, uniform, unfiltered. Lab 4: NaCl in water. Hotter water dissolves more.
Suspension heterogeneous mixture
Particles too large to dissolve — cloudy, settles, filterable. Lab 4: flour in water. Oats (#64) before cooking. Heat triggers gelatinization (~65°C).
Emulsion new in lab 5
New. Oil and water forced together by an emulsifier — a molecule that bridges polar and nonpolar phases. Lab 4: oil separated because there was no emulsifier. Peanut butter (#36) provides one. Warm = less stable.
Heterogeneous Mixture new in lab 5
New. Components stay visibly separate — nothing dissolves or disperses. Trail mix, grain bowls, parfait layers. No heat needed — the default for cold and room-temperature recipes.

Electrolytes in Food
2 pts
Building on Lab 2: In Lab 2 you classified minerals as macrominerals (needed in hundreds of mg per day — Ca, P, K, Na, Mg) or microminerals (needed in microgram-to-mg amounts — Fe, Zn, Cu, I, Se). Every macromineral on that list is an electrolyte in the body. When they dissolve in water and break into ions, they carry charge — which is exactly what makes them essential for nerve and muscle function.

Electrolytes are substances that break apart into ions when dissolved in water. The resulting charged particles allow the solution to conduct electricity and are essential for bodily functions including nerve signaling, muscle contraction, and fluid balance.

NaCl → Na⁺ + Cl⁻  (iodized salt, #59)
K⁺ from bananas (#21) — stored as potassium salts in plant cell fluid
Ca²⁺ from Greek yogurt (#43) — ionized calcium in dairy
Mg²⁺ from almonds (#40) — released during digestion

Nutrition Chemistry Preview
2 pts
Building on Lab 2: In Lab 2 you studied minerals — inorganic elements the body needs in small amounts. Macronutrients are different: they are organic molecules needed in large amounts (grams, not milligrams) that provide energy and structural material. Don’t confuse macrominerals (Ca, Mg, K…) with macronutrients (carbohydrates, proteins, fats) — both use the prefix because both are needed in relatively large quantities, but they are entirely different categories.

Macronutrients each play specific roles. Carbohydrates (polysaccharides like starch in oats, simple sugars like glucose and fructose in honey) are the body’s primary energy source. Proteins (chains of amino acids) build and repair tissue. Fats (triglycerides composed of fatty acids) are essential for fat-soluble vitamin absorption (A, D, E, K), brain function, and sustained energy.
