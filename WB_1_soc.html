The Council Wants Answers

Item 1 — Classify the Audit Elements
6 pts
How to Use the Table for This Item

Metals fill the left and center of the table (Groups 1–12 plus lower-left of Groups 13–16). They tend to lose electrons. Nonmetals cluster in the upper right (Groups 15–18 plus hydrogen). They tend to gain or share electrons. Metalloids run along the staircase boundary (B, Si, Ge, As, Sb, Te, At) and show properties of both.

Audit Report — Section B
Water Infrastructure: Treatment Chemistry and Reaction Types
The audit flagged two chemical processes at Bracket City's water treatment facility. First, inspectors found that magnesium anodes used to protect the steel tanks had partially oxidized — magnesium metal reacting with oxygen in the air to form a white powdery solid. Second, the treatment plant uses additional chemical reactions to make the water safe to drink. Understanding these reactions — and being able to write and balance them — is essential for assessing what is actually entering the water supply.

Item 4 — The Oxidized Anodes
8 pts
Balancing Equations — Quick Reference

A balanced equation has the same number of each type of atom on both sides of the arrow. Adjust coefficients (the numbers in front of each formula) only — never change subscripts inside a formula. A coefficient of 1 is usually omitted but you should enter it explicitly here. The simplest whole-number ratio is the standard form.

4A The facility's magnesium anodes are oxidizing. Magnesium (Mg) reacts with oxygen gas (O2) in the air to form magnesium oxide (MgO), the white powder found coating the tank walls. Identify the correct word equation for this reaction. 2 pts

A word equation names the reactants (left of the arrow) and products (right of the arrow) in plain language. Select one term for each blank.

4B Now balance the formula equation. Enter whole-number coefficients for each substance. The equation is: __ Mg  +  __ O2  →  __ MgO 6 pts

Count Mg atoms and O atoms on both sides. Adjust one coefficient at a time until both sides match. The simplest whole-number ratio earns full credit.

Item 6 — Decode the Label Compounds
8 pts
Nomenclature Quick Reference

Ionic compounds form between a metal and a nonmetal (or polyatomic ion). Name the metal first, then the nonmetal with an -ide suffix (or use the polyatomic ion name as given). Covalent compounds form between two nonmetals. Use prefixes (mono-, di-, tri-...) to indicate how many of each atom are present.
