Bracket City — Active Cases

Three Water Quality Scenarios

Your Lab 4 investigation and Workbook 4 calculations center on these three confirmed situations. The chemistry you apply this week directly informs whether the Food Bank can safely operate.

Case 1 — Food Bank Well Water
Simulated Contamination Sample
The Food Bank’s well water shows visible cloudiness and an oily surface film. Your lab creates a simulated sample with known contaminants to test purification methods and calculate concentration relative to EPA safe limits.
Case 2 — Food Bank Bottled Water Supply
Bottled Versus Tap Water Decision
A donor has offered to fund either a bottled water supply or a tap water filtration system for the Food Bank kitchen. Inspectors must compare the dissolved solid content, mineral composition, and safety records of both sources to make an evidence-based recommendation — a decision that affects every meal prepared on site.
Case 3 — Community Kitchen Water Supply
Electrolytes & Water Safety for Food Preparation
Water used in the community kitchen must meet food-grade standards for dissolved electrolytes. Recent testing flagged elevated sodium and chloride ion concentrations from a nearby road salt runoff event. Inspectors need to evaluate the dissolved ion data and determine whether the water is safe for cooking and drinking by vulnerable populations.
Case 4 — Food Bank Well Water
Hard Water & Dissolved Ionic Compounds
The Food Bank’s well water has a visible mineral residue on cookware and equipment. Testing confirms elevated concentrations of dissolved calcium and magnesium ions — hallmarks of hard water. Inspectors must calculate the dissolved ion concentration in ppm and determine whether the water meets safe limits for food preparation before the kitchen can reopen.

Section V

Learning Objectives

By the end of Week 5, you will be able to:

  1. Balance chemical equations using atom counting and conservation of mass
  2. Identify five reaction types: synthesis, decomposition, single replacement, double replacement, combustion
  3. Write balanced equations for reactions relevant to food chemistry and water treatment
  4. Use mole ratios from balanced equations to calculate amounts of reactants and products
  5. Convert between grams, moles, and number of molecules using molar mass and Avogadro’s number
  6. Apply stoichiometry to calculate theoretical yields and quantities needed for reactions
  7. Determine limiting reactants when given amounts of multiple reactants
  8. Calculate contaminant concentrations in ppm and evaluate against EPA safe limits
Section VI

Common Questions

I have both Lab 3 and Lab 4 work this week — how do I manage the load?
Lab 3 data is already collected from last week — this week you are writing the report. Treat it as a Monday or Tuesday task so the rest of the week is dedicated to Lab 4. The timeline above is structured with this sequence in mind. If Lab 3 still needs significant work, prioritize that before starting Lab 4 experiments.
What materials do I need for Lab 4?
Lab 4 uses common household materials — salt, sugar, vegetable oil, flour, a food scale, coffee filters, and measuring cups. Most of these were already on the Week 1 materials list. Check the Lab 4 instructions for the complete list and quantities before you begin.
How does the stoichiometry in Workbook 4 connect to Lab 4?
Lab 4 gives you the experimental side — what physical methods can and cannot remove from water. Workbook 4 gives you the mathematical side — calculating how much of a substance is present and what reactions would be needed to neutralize or remove it. Together they give you a complete picture of the water quality problem facing Bracket City.
When is the full Lab 4 report due?
The data tables and calculations are due at the end of Week 5. The full written report is due at the end of Week 6, giving you time to write a thorough analysis after completing the experimental work.
