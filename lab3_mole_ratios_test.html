Lab Scenario — Reaction Stoichiometry
How do atoms combine? Discovering mole ratios by calorimetry.
In chemistry, a balanced equation tells us the exact whole-number ratio in which reactants combine and products form. But what if we did not already know the balanced equation? The method of continuous variations lets us discover that ratio experimentally by systematically changing the amounts of two reactants while keeping the total volume constant — and measuring the energy released in each trial. The trial producing the greatest temperature change reveals the ratio at which the two reactants are perfectly matched: no excess of either, maximum reaction.
Your Mission: Mix hypochlorite (OCl–) and thiosulfate (S2O32–) in seven different volume ratios. Measure the temperature change for each trial. Identify the ratio that produces the largest temperature change — that is the experimental mole ratio. Compare your result to the known balanced equation.

II
Background & Key Concepts
Read Before Lab
Why This Experiment Matters

Stoichiometry — the quantitative relationship between reactants and products — underlies virtually every application of chemistry, from pharmaceutical synthesis to industrial manufacturing. The method of continuous variations (also called Job's method) is a powerful technique used in both teaching and research to determine reaction ratios without knowing the balanced equation in advance.

The Mole

A mole is 6.022 × 1023 particles (Avogadro's number). It is the chemist's "counting unit" for atoms and molecules, allowing us to work with macroscopic (measurable) amounts of substances.

1 mol NaCl = 58.44 g NaCl

Mole Ratio

The coefficients in a balanced chemical equation give the mole ratio of reactants and products. If a reaction is A + 2B → products, for every 1 mol of A consumed, exactly 2 mol of B are consumed.

2H2 + O2 → 2H2O   ratio = 2:1

Limiting Reactant

The reactant that is completely consumed first controls how much product forms. The other reactant is in excess. Maximum reaction occurs when both reactants are perfectly matched by the mole ratio — no limiting reactant.

Excess = no maximum energy release

Continuous Variations

Keep total volume constant. Change the volume ratio of two reactants across many trials. The trial with the greatest temperature change corresponds to the exact mole ratio of the reaction — neither reactant is limiting.

VA + VB = constant (10.0 mL)

Molarity (Concentration)

Molarity (M) = moles of solute per liter of solution. If both solutions have the same molarity, the volume ratio is directly equal to the mole ratio, because moles = M × V.

mol = M × V(L)    if MA = MB

Exothermic Reactions

A reaction that releases heat to the surroundings is exothermic. The solution temperature increases. The maximum temperature increase (greatest ΔT) signals the trial where energy release is maximized — i.e., the stoichiometric ratio.

ΔT = Tfinal − Tinitial > 0
The Reactants in This Experiment

Hypochlorite ion, OCl–: The active ingredient in household bleach. A strong oxidizing agent that accepts electrons from other species. Common in bleach-based disinfectants and pool chemicals.

Thiosulfate ion, S2O32–: A reducing agent used in photography (as "fixer") and in analytical chemistry to titrate iodine. Donates electrons to oxidizing agents like OCl–.

8
Repeat steps 3–7 for all 7 trials. Try to keep the experimental conditions as consistent as possible between trials (same room temperature, same technique, same mixing speed).
Work efficiently — the reaction proceeds quickly once mixed. Having both solutions measured and ready before initiating mixing improves reproducibility.

Question 3

Start with a trial that used a small volume of OCl– (e.g., Trial 1: 2 mL OCl– / 8 mL S2O32–). Then pick a trial that used a large volume of OCl– (e.g., Trial 7: 8 mL / 2 mL). How to Decide

The balanced equation tells us 4 moles of OCl– react with 1 mole of S2O32–. That means OCl– must make up about 80% of the total volume (8 out of 10 mL) to be in the right proportion. In trials where OCl– is less than 80% of the total, there is not enough OCl– — it is the limiting reactant. In trials where OCl– is more than 80%, there is not enough S2O32– — it becomes the limiting reactant.

CV
Method of Continuous Variations (Job's Method)
Stoichiometry determination technique
Core Method

The method of continuous variations determines the mole ratio of reactants by holding the total amount of reactants constant and systematically varying how much of each reactant is used across a series of trials.

The key insight: reaction produces the most heat (or product) when the reactants are mixed in exactly the stoichiometric ratio. At this ratio, neither reactant is in excess — both are completely consumed, giving maximum energy release. Trials on either side have one reactant in excess, which is wasted and reduces the temperature change.

Design Constraint
VA + VB = Vtotal = constant
Vary: VA from small to large    (simultaneously VB decreases)
Measure: ΔT for each trial    Find: VA* at max ΔT
M
Molarity, Moles, and Volume
Quantitative solution chemistry
Calculation
Molarity Definition
M = n / V    [mol/L]
Rearranged: n = M × V    [mol]
When MA = MB:   nA/nB = VA/VB
This equality (mole ratio = volume ratio when molarities are equal) is what makes this experiment work. If molarities were different, you would need to correct for concentration: nA/nB = (MA × VA)/(MB × VB).
LR
Limiting Reactant & Excess Reactant
Stoichiometry concept
Key Concept

The limiting reactant is the one that runs out first. Once it is consumed, the reaction stops — no matter how much of the other reactant remains. The amount of product formed is determined by the limiting reactant.

The excess reactant is present in greater than the stoichiometric amount. Leftover after the reaction is complete. Having excess reactant does NOT produce more product — it is simply wasted.

In this experiment: trials with small V(OCl–) have OCl– as limiting reactant. Trials with large V(OCl–) have S2O32– as limiting. The maximum ΔT trial has neither — exact stoichiometric proportion.

How to Identify Limiting Reactant
Compare moles available vs. moles required:
Required ratio = a:b (from balanced equation)
If nA/nB < a/b  →  A is limiting
If nA/nB > a/b  →  B is limiting
dT
Calorimetry & Temperature Measurement
Heat measurement technique
Measurement

Calorimetry measures heat exchange between a chemical reaction and its surroundings. In a Styrofoam cup calorimeter, heat lost to the environment is minimized, so the temperature change of the solution approximates the heat released by the reaction.

Temperature Change
ΔT = Tfinal − Tinitial    (°C)
Exothermic reaction: ΔT > 0 (solution warms)
Endothermic reaction: ΔT < 0 (solution cools)

For greatest accuracy: ensure both solutions are at the same initial temperature, record Tinitial for each solution individually and average them, and record Tfinal as the highest (plateau) temperature before cooling begins.

The OCl– / S2O32– Reaction
Oxidation-reduction chemistry
Reference Rxn
4 OCl–  +  1 S2O32–  +  H2O  →  4 Cl–  +  SO42–  +  SO32–  +  2H+

Type of reaction: Redox (oxidation-reduction). OCl– is the oxidizing agent (it is reduced, gaining electrons). S2O32– is the reducing agent (it is oxidized, losing electrons).

Mole ratio: 4 mol OCl– per 1 mol S2O32–. This is the ratio you are trying to confirm experimentally. Your result should fall near 4:1, or equivalently 8:2 or 80%:20% of total volume.

Products: Cl– (chloride ion), sulfate (SO42–), sulfite (SO32–), and hydrogen ions (H+). The reaction proceeds readily at room temperature and is exothermic.
