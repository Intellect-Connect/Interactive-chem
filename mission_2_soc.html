Bracket City — Mission 2: Elements in the Food Supply
Who Put Lead in the Peanut Butter?
Welcome back, Food Safety Chemist. Last week you used density measurements to flag mislabeled and adulterated liquid shipments — and that work mattered. Three containers that arrived labeled as olive oil tested at densities inconsistent with any pure vegetable oil. They were pulled from distribution before reaching a single household. That is what good chemistry does in a food safety context: it catches what eyes and labels miss.

This week the problem is harder to see. Bracket City’s testing lab has returned results on a batch of packaged and processed foods from the inventory, and several samples show elevated levels of heavy metals — lead, arsenic, cadmium, and mercury. These are not accidental. They can enter the food supply through contaminated soil, industrial runoff, unsafe packaging materials, or deliberate adulteration. Unlike spoiled food, you cannot smell or taste them. A jar of peanut butter laced with lead looks identical to a safe one. A bag of rice with elevated arsenic from contaminated groundwater smells perfectly fine. The only way to know is to test — and to understand the chemistry well enough to interpret what the results mean.

But here is what makes this investigation genuinely complicated: not every metal in food is a threat. Iron, calcium, zinc, magnesium — these are essential nutrients your body cannot function without, and the periodic table is the map that tells you which elements belong in the human diet and which ones have no business being there. Your job today is to learn that map, apply it to real foods from the Bracket City inventory, and use it to make distribution decisions that protect residents — especially children, whose developing bodies absorb heavy metals at higher rates and suffer more serious long-term harm.
Your Mission: Research essential and toxic elements, color-code the periodic table to reveal the chemistry behind which elements belong in food and which don't, analyze your own food's nutrition label, and use the Bracket City inventory to make evidence-based distribution recommendations for 5,000 residents — especially children.

Real-World Context — Why This Matters

In 2021, a congressional investigation found that major baby food brands — including Gerber and Beech-Nut — had internal test results showing toxic metal contamination and kept selling the products anyway. The FDA launched its Closer to Zero program in response. As of 2025, no binding legal limits exist for lead, arsenic, cadmium, or mercury in most children’s food. [2021 House Subcommittee Report] [FDA — Closer to Zero]

In fall 2023, over 200 children in 33 states were poisoned by lead-contaminated cinnamon applesauce pouches sold at major retailers. Lead had been added during overseas processing and wasn’t caught until children were already harmed. Scientists agree: there is no safe level of lead exposure for a developing brain. Even small amounts can permanently lower IQ and affect behavior. [CDC — 2023 Applesauce Outbreak]

These risks fall hardest on low-income families who rely on a narrower set of foods with less nutritional variety. When the body is short on an essential mineral, it absorbs more of whatever is chemically similar — including toxic metals. A child deficient in calcium absorbs more lead. A child low in iron takes up more arsenic. Nutrient deficiency and toxic exposure don’t cancel each other out — they compound. [Healthy People 2030 — Food Insecurity]

The periodic table explains why. Elements in the same column behave alike in the body, which is how lead mimics calcium and arsenic mimics phosphate. Reading a nutrition label through a chemistry lens lets you see what’s present, what’s missing, and what the label isn’t required to tell you — exactly the skill used by food safety researchers and public health workers protecting communities like Bracket City. [PMC — Toxic Mechanisms of Heavy Metals]

The Periodic Table & Food Safety
Concept Review
Why the Periodic Table Matters Here

Arsenic doesn't sneak into a cell by accident — it gets in because arsenic and phosphorus are so chemically alike that the cell can't tell them apart. Both sit in Group 15 of the periodic table, which means they have the same number of outer electrons and bond in the same way. When arsenic forms a compound in water, it takes on a shape and charge nearly identical to phosphate — the form phosphorus takes inside the body. The transport proteins that pull phosphate into cells for DNA, ATP, and bone treat the arsenic version of that compound exactly the same way. Understanding this kind of ionic mimicry — where a toxic element impersonates a nutrient at the molecular level — is exactly why chemists study the periodic table. An element's group tells you how it behaves, which toxins it resembles, and why certain poisons are so hard for the body to screen out.

Element Symbol

A unique 1–2 letter shorthand for each element. Every box on the periodic table has one, and you will see them on nutrition labels, chemical formulas, and lab reports.

P = Phosphorus; As = Arsenic

Atomic Number

The number of protons in each atom — this defines the element. No two elements share the same atomic number.

P: Z = 15; As: Z = 33

Group (Column)

Elements in the same group share similar chemical behavior — same outer electron count, similar reactivity. Arsenic mimics phosphorus because both are Group 15 and form ions that enzymes cannot tell apart.

As & P: both Group 15

Essential Nutritional Elements
Mark BLUE on Periodic Table
Not Getting Enough Makes Things Worse

Here's something that often surprises people: being low on a nutrient can actually make toxic exposure more dangerous, not less. When your body is running low on phosphorus, it increases uptake through phosphate transporters — and arsenic gets in through those same routes, because it forms a compound that looks chemically identical to phosphate. When your body is running low on calcium, it ramps up absorption through calcium transport channels — and lead slips in through those same channels, because Pb2+ and Ca2+ carry the same charge and a similar size. So a child who isn't getting enough phosphorus or calcium doesn't just face a deficiency. They also become more vulnerable to the toxic metals that exploit those exact same pathways. Deficiency and contamination aren't separate problems — they amplify each other. That's the core public health issue this lab is built around.

Macrominerals

Needed in relatively large amounts — hundreds of milligrams to grams per day. Think of these as the structural and electrical workhorses: bones, fluid balance, muscle contraction, nerve signals.

Microminerals (Trace Minerals)

Needed in tiny amounts — micrograms to a few milligrams per day. Small dose does not mean less important: a trace amount of iodine is all that stands between normal thyroid function and a goiter.

Toxic Contaminant Elements
Mark RED on Periodic Table
These Are the Four From the Headlines

Lead, arsenic, mercury, and cadmium are the exact elements at the center of the 2021 Congressional baby food investigation. Lead alone was responsible for the 2023 cinnamon applesauce recall, in which lead chromate — used to artificially color cinnamon powder — contaminated products that sickened more than 500 children across 44 states. None of these four elements have any job in the body — they're not nutrients, they serve no function, and there is no safe amount for a child to consume. You'll never see them listed on a Nutrition Facts label, even if they're present. They get into food through contaminated soil, irrigation water, industrial pollution, and sometimes through deliberate adulteration. A child's body absorbs up to 50% of the lead it ingests — compared to about 10% in adults — which is why children are so much more vulnerable to the damage these elements cause.

Reading Nutrition Labels Chemically
Label Literacy
Labels Show You Some Things — But Not Everything

Here's something worth knowing before you ever pick up a food label again: toxic heavy metals will never appear on a Nutrition Facts panel, even if they're in the food. If an element has a % Daily Value listed, that means it's recognized as a nutrient your body needs. Lead, arsenic, mercury, and cadmium have no Daily Value because they have no nutritional role — so the label has no place to put them, and no requirement to disclose them. Fortified foods intentionally add beneficial minerals and list them; contamination enters silently and isn't disclosed anywhere. Learning to read labels chemically means understanding both what you see and what the label was never designed to tell you.

Fortified vs. Whole Foods
Fortified Foods
Nutrients have been intentionally added to increase nutritional value. Look for chemical compound names in the ingredient list: "ferrous sulfate," "calcium carbonate," "zinc oxide," "ferric orthophosphate," "reduced iron."
Whole Foods
Minerals present are naturally occurring within the food's biological structure. Nutrient amounts are typically lower per serving. These may spoil faster due to moisture, enzymatic activity, and cellular structure.

Part A — Mark each statement TRUE or FALSE. Each one connects chemistry from this lab to real decisions in the Bracket City inventory.
Arsenic (As) is toxic partly because it shares Group 15 with phosphorus (P). In water, arsenic forms arsenate — a compound with the same charge and nearly identical shape as phosphate. This is why the body’s phosphate transport proteins pull arsenic into cells, and why arsenic contamination in rice and root vegetables is especially dangerous for infants whose cells are actively building DNA and ATP.
Lead (Pb) enters the body through calcium transport channels because Pb2+ and Ca2+ carry the same charge and a similar ionic size. This means a child who is calcium-deficient — with upregulated calcium absorption — will absorb more lead from the same food than a well-nourished child. Nutrient deficiency and toxic contamination are therefore not independent risks.
Because cadmium (Cd) and zinc (Zn) are both in Group 12 and share similar ionic radii, cadmium mimics zinc in enzyme binding sites. This means cadmium is only dangerous when zinc intake is low — if a person gets enough zinc, cadmium cannot displace it and poses no health risk.
A fortified food always delivers more bioavailable minerals than the equivalent whole food, because fortification adds measured, standardized amounts of compounds like ferrous sulfate directly to the product.
