Concept Check Questions

Answer these questions to test your understanding of heating curves and phase transitions. Click your answer choice to check if you're correct.

1. During a phase transition (horizontal plateau), the temperature remains constant because:
A No heat is being added to the substance
B The added energy is used to overcome intermolecular forces, not increase kinetic energy
C The substance loses heat at the same rate it gains heat
D Intermolecular forces prevent the temperature from changing
2. A steeper slope in a heating region (where temperature increases) indicates that the substance:
A Has a higher specific heat capacity and requires more energy per degree of temperature change
B Has a lower specific heat capacity and requires less energy per degree of temperature change
C Is undergoing a phase transition
D Has stronger intermolecular forces in that phase
3. Sodium chloride (NaCl) has a much higher boiling point (1413°C) than water (100°C) because:
A NaCl has a higher molar mass than water
B Ionic interactions in NaCl are much stronger than hydrogen bonding in water, requiring more energy to separate particles
C NaCl molecules are larger than water molecules
D Salt dissolves in water, which raises the boiling point
4. Why is the enthalpy of vaporization (ΔHvap) always larger than the enthalpy of fusion (ΔHfus) for the same substance?
A Vaporization occurs at a higher temperature than melting
B Vaporization requires completely breaking all intermolecular forces, while melting only weakens them partially
C Gas molecules move faster than liquid molecules
D The gas phase has a higher specific heat capacity than the liquid phase
5. Chlorine (Cl₂) has a boiling point of -34°C while sulfur dioxide (SO₂) has a boiling point of -10°C. Both have similar molar masses. This difference is primarily because:
A Cl₂ is a larger molecule than SO₂
B SO₂ is polar (dipole-dipole forces) while Cl₂ is nonpolar (London dispersion only)
C Cl₂ has hydrogen bonding while SO₂ does not
D SO₂ has a higher specific heat capacity
6. In the sloped regions of the heating curve (where temperature increases), the added heat energy primarily goes into:
A Breaking intermolecular forces and converting to potential energy
B Increasing the kinetic energy of particles (faster molecular motion)
C Changing the phase of the substance
D Increasing the volume of the substance
7. You need to calculate the total energy required to convert 50 g of ice at -20°C to steam at 120°C. Which quantities from the heating curve do you need?
A Only the specific heat capacities for ice, water, and steam
B Only ΔHfus and ΔHvap
C The specific heat capacities for all three phases, plus ΔHfus and ΔHvap
D The melting and boiling points only
