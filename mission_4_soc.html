The Water Report No One Wanted to Write
Welcome back, Food Safety Chemist. In Lab 3 you pulled four toxic compounds from Bracket City’s food supply — formaldehyde in imported seafood, methanol in counterfeit vanilla extract, hydrogen sulfide from decomposing warehouse stock, carbon monoxide leaking from faulty equipment. Each one was dangerous because of its molecular shape and how that shape interacted with the chemistry of the body. You identified them. You made the call. The shelves are safer because of it.

But the investigation isn’t over. Bracket City’s food bank uses well water for nearly everything that happens in that building: washing produce, cooking rice and beans, preparing soups, sterilizing cutting surfaces, and cleaning the equipment used to package and distribute food to 5,000 residents. That water comes from a well located near active farmland, and recent heavy rainfall has driven agricultural runoff directly into the groundwater table. Fertilizer residue. Sediment. Pesticide residue. Possibly fuel. No one is certain what is in it — only that something has changed. The water that looked clear last month is not behaving the same way today.

This week’s problem is structurally different from the previous three missions. Before, you were identifying a specific substance — a mismatched density, a toxic element, a dangerous compound. This time, the threat is not a single thing. It is a behavior: how substances interact with water. Some contaminants dissolve completely and become invisible to the eye — you would drink them without knowing. Some form cloudy suspensions of particles that refuse to settle. Some are nonpolar and do not mix at all, floating in a separate layer on the surface. Each type requires a different purification approach, and not every method that looks like it is working actually is. Settling removes particles but leaves dissolved salts. Filtration catches sediment but passes right over anything ionic. Dilution spreads the problem without solving it. Your job is to figure out which method actually works — and to write a recommendation the food bank can afford to act on.
Your Mission: Test the food bank’s water to determine what is dissolved versus suspended. Figure out which purification methods (settling, filtration, dilution) actually work — and which ones only look like they are working. Then recommend a realistic, affordable solution the food bank can use.
Real-World Context — Why This Matters

At a food bank, water is not just for drinking — it is used to wash produce, cook meals like rice and beans, and keep surfaces clean and safe. When the water is contaminated, that contamination ends up in every meal served. In many rural areas across the U.S., well water is affected by runoff from nearby farms, which can carry fertilizers, pesticides, and soil into the water supply — often without anyone realizing it has happened. [EPA — Well Water Contaminants] [CDC — Private Well Health Risks]

The biggest problem: contaminated water usually looks fine. Many harmful substances dissolve completely — no color, no smell, no visible sign anything is wrong. The EPA sets a safety limit of 500 ppm (parts per million) for total dissolved solids in drinking water, but that standard applies only to regulated public water systems. About 45 million Americans rely on private wells with no mandatory testing and no federal oversight — and rural and low-income communities are disproportionately among them. A food bank drawing from a private well near fertilized farmland has no external agency verifying whether its water is safe. The only way to know is to test, and most lack the equipment or budget to do it. [EPA — Drinking Water Regulations] [GAO — Private Wells Report (2021)] [USGS — Agricultural Water Contaminants]

Why Some Things Dissolve — and Others Don’t
The Key Principle
Like Dissolves Like

Water (H2O) has a bent shape, and its electrons are pulled strongly toward the oxygen end — creating a molecule with a negative side (oxygen) and a positive side (hydrogens). That charge imbalance is what makes water polar, and it is exactly what makes it such a powerful solvent.

The rule in chemistry is simple: polar substances dissolve in polar solvents, and nonpolar substances dissolve in nonpolar solvents. Since water is polar, it is an excellent solvent for other polar things — and a terrible solvent for nonpolar things.

Measuring Water Quality: Parts Per Million (ppm)
Concentration
Why We Use ppm
When chemists test water, they need a way to express how much of a substance is present in a very large amount of water. Parts per million (ppm) is the standard unit for this. Think of it like this: out of one million parts of water, how many parts are the contaminant?
ppm = (mass of substance / mass of water) × 1,000,000
Example Calculation

If you dissolve 1 gram of salt in 1,000 grams of water:

ppm = (1 ÷ 1,000) × 1,000,000 = 1,000 ppm

The EPA recommends that safe drinking water have fewer than 500 ppm of total dissolved solids. In this lab, your test water will likely exceed that limit significantly — which is exactly the point.

Three Purification Methods You Will Test
Methods Overview
Method How It Works What It Removes What It Cannot Remove
Settling Let water sit undisturbed. Heavy particles sink to the bottom; lighter ones (like oil) float to the top. Dense suspended particles (flour, sand) Dissolved substances (salt, vinegar) and light substances (oil floats)
Filtration Pour water through a filter. Particles larger than the filter pores are trapped on the filter. Suspended particles (larger than filter holes) Dissolved substances — they are too small to trap
Dilution Add clean water. Concentration decreases even though total amount of contaminant stays the same. Nothing is actually removed Everything — dilution does not remove anything, only spreads it out

pH and Water Quality
Acidity & Alkalinity
What Is pH?

pH is a measure of how acidic or alkaline (basic) a solution is. The scale runs from 0 to 14. A pH of 7.0 is neutral — pure water at room temperature. Values below 7 are acidic (more H⁺ ions), and values above 7 are alkaline (more OH⁻ ions). Each whole number on the scale represents a 10-fold change in ion concentration, so pH 5 is 10 times more acidic than pH 6 and 100 times more acidic than pH 7.

The pH Formula

pH is defined mathematically as the negative base-10 logarithm of the hydrogen ion concentration:

pH = −log[H+]

[H+] is the concentration of hydrogen ions in solution, measured in moles per liter (mol/L). When a substance releases H+ ions into water, it acts as an acid and lowers pH. When it releases OH− ions (or absorbs H+), it acts as a base and raises pH.

[H+] concentration pH value Example
0.001 mol/L (10−3) 3 Vinegar, acid rain
0.0000001 mol/L (10−7) 7 Pure water (neutral)
0.00000000001 mol/L (10−11) 11 Ammonia cleaner, bleach

Notice that you do not need to calculate pH yourself in this lab — you read it directly from a color-matched strip. Understanding the formula helps you interpret why a half-unit shift (e.g., pH 7 to 6.5) actually means a threefold increase in acidity.

Part 3 — Test Method 2: Filtration
10–15 min
Food Bank Context
Filtration costs more than settling but may remove more. Weighing the filter before and after tells you exactly how much material was physically trapped.
1
Place a dry coffee filter on your scale and record its mass in Data Table 2 (Filter mass BEFORE).
2
Set the filter in a funnel (or fold it into a cone) over a clean cup labeled “Filtered Water.”
3
Stir the main test water sample again, then pour approximately 100 mL slowly through the filter. Let it drain completely — this may take 5–10 minutes.
Pour slowly and steadily. Rushing causes the filter to overflow and bypass, which means particles that should be caught pass straight through.
4
Let the filter dry for at least 5 minutes, then weigh it again. Record the new mass in Data Table 2 (Filter mass AFTER). The difference is the mass trapped.
5
Observe the filtered water: Is it clear, cloudy, or colored? Does oil remain? Record all observations and check the substance matrix in Data Table 2.
6
pH check: Dip a strip into the filtered water. Read and record as Filtered water in Data Table 4. Did filtration change the pH compared to the test water? Why or why not?
Filtration only removes particles large enough to be physically trapped. Dissolved species (salt, acetic acid from vinegar) pass straight through the filter, so pH should remain acidic after filtration — filtration cannot remove dissolved acids.
7
Photograph the dirty filter and the filtered water side by side. Both must be visible in the same frame, or submit as two photos together. Upload to Canvas.
The filter photo shows what was physically removed. The water photo shows what passed through. Together they make the key scientific point: filtration is selective — it catches particles but not dissolved substances.

Part 4 — Test Method 3: Dilution Calculation
Calculation Only
No physical experiment needed — just math
For this part, you will use the dilution formula M₁V₁ = M₂V₂ to determine how much clean water would need to be added to bring your test water down to the EPA’s safe limit of 500 ppm. Complete this calculation in Data Table 3 on the Data Tables tab.
Dilution Formula

M₁V₁ = M₂V₂ where M is concentration (ppm) and V is volume in mL

M₁ = your measured concentration from Part 1 (ppm)
V₁ = volume of your test water in mL — since 1 mL of dilute water ≈ 1 g, use your scale reading directly (e.g. 120 g → enter 120 mL)
M₂ = 500 ppm (EPA safe limit, your target)
V₂ = the total volume of the final mixture — the Data Table 3 calculator solves this for you

Your step: Clean water to add (mL) = V₂ − V₁ — subtract and enter your answer in Data Table 3. The lab will convert it to gallons.
