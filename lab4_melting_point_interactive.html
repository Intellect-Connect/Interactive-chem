What makes a melting point useful for identifying a substance?
Every pure substance has a characteristic melting point — a narrow, reproducible temperature at which it transitions from solid to liquid. In this lab you will measure the melting point of a known compound (naphthalene) to practice the technique, then measure an unknown solid and use its melting point to narrow down its identity. Finally, you will prepare a mixture of your unknown and your best candidate — if the melting points match, the mixture test confirms your identification.

II
Background
Key Concepts
Melting Point

The temperature at which a solid and its liquid coexist in equilibrium. A pure substance melts over a narrow range (0.5–1.0 °C). An impure substance melts lower and over a broader range.

Melting Point Range

Recorded as start (first drop of liquid) to end (last crystal disappears). Sharp range = pure. Broad range (>2 °C) = impurity or mixture.

Mixed Melting Point

Same compound + same compound = no change. Different compound mixed in = melting point drops and broadens. This mixed melting point depression is the definitive identity test.

Physical Property

Melting point is an intensive property — it does not depend on sample size. It is a characteristic “fingerprint” of the substance.

Heating Rate

Heat too fast and you overshoot the melting point. Slow to ~1–2 °C/min as you approach the expected value. Two runs allow technique refinement.

Identity Confirmation

Identification requires two criteria: (1) experimental m.p. matches Table 1 within ±2 °C, AND (2) the mixture test produces a sharp, unchanged melting point.

Procedure
Step by Step
Safety

Naphthalene is volatile with a strong odor — work in a fume hood or well-ventilated area. Avoid inhalation and skin contact. Unknown solids should be treated as potentially irritating until identified. The melting point apparatus becomes hot — allow surfaces to cool before handling.

  1. 1
    Load the capillary tube. Press the open end into a small pile of dry solid several times to pack a 2–3 mm plug into the closed end. Tap gently on the bench to settle the sample.
    A tightly packed, 2–3 mm plug gives the sharpest results. A loose or oversized sample gives a broad, inaccurate range.
  2. 2
    Insert into the melting point apparatus. Place the capillary in the sample well. Set the starting temperature approximately 10–15 °C below the expected melting point.
  3. 3
    Heat rapidly, then slowly. Increase temperature quickly until ~10 °C below the expected melting point, then slow the rate to 1–2 °C/min.
    Slow heating near the melting point is critical. Fast heating causes the thermometer to lag and gives a reading that is too high.
  4. 4
    Record start and end of melting. Note the temperature when the first drop of liquid appears (start) and when the last crystal disappears (end). This is your melting point range.
  5. 5
    Cool and run a second trial. Let temperature drop at least 10 °C below the melting point before loading a new capillary. Run a second trial with the same technique.
  6. 6
    Repeat for the unknown solid. Follow steps 1–5 for your assigned unknown sample.
  7. 7
    Prepare and measure the mixture. Mix roughly equal amounts of the unknown and your best candidate from Table 1. Grind in a mortar, load a new capillary, and measure the melting point. A sharp, unchanged melting point confirms identity; a depressed or broad melting point means the compounds are different.
    If two or three candidates are close in melting point, prepare a separate mixture for each.

m.p.
Melting Point & Purity
+

The melting point is the temperature at which the solid and liquid phases of a substance coexist in equilibrium. It is an intensive property — it does not depend on the amount of substance.

Pure substance: Melts sharply over 0.5–1.0 °C. This narrow range is the hallmark of high purity.

Impure substance or mixture: Melts lower than the pure compound and over a broader range (often 5–10 °C or more). Impurities disrupt the crystal lattice, lowering the melting point (freezing point depression).

A melting point can confirm the identity of a known substance, assess purity, and (combined with mixture testing) identify an unknown.

ΔT
Recording a Melting Point Range
+

Always record melting point as a range: from the temperature at which the first drop of liquid appears to the temperature at which the last crystal disappears.

Sharp range (0.5–1.0 °C): Pure substance.

Moderate range (1–2 °C): Acceptable for introductory labs.

Broad range (>2 °C): Impurity, contamination, a mixture, or too-fast heating.

The average (midpoint of start and end) is used to compare against literature values.

MIX
Mixed Melting Point Test
+

The mixed melting point test distinguishes between two substances with the same or similar melting points.

Procedure: Mix roughly equal amounts of unknown and candidate known compound. Grind well in a mortar. Load into a capillary and measure the melting point of the mixture.

▸ Same compound → mixture melts at the same temperature with a sharp range.

▸ Different compound → mixture melts at a lower temperature with a broad range (melting point depression).

This test is conclusive even when two different substances happen to have very similar melting points.

ERR
Sources of Error
+

Too-fast heating rate: Most common error. The thermometer lags behind the true sample temperature, giving a reading that is too high.

Apparatus not cooled between runs: Starting Run 2 before cooling at least 10 °C below the melting point gives inaccurate results.

Loosely packed sample: Air gaps in the capillary cause erratic, broad melting ranges.

Sample contamination: Even small amounts of a different substance lower and broaden the melting point.

Wet sample: Residual moisture lowers the melting point.

Thermometer calibration: Uncalibrated thermometers introduce systematic error — this is why your two runs should agree with each other even if both deviate from the literature value.
