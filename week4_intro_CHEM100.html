Bracket City — Active Cases

The Four Molecular Threats

Your Lab 3 investigation centers on these four confirmed contamination events. Understanding the molecular structure of each compound is what connects the chemistry to the crisis.

Case 1 — Seafood Shipment S-471
Formaldehyde Contamination
Detected at 127 ppm in imported fish. 320 lbs distributed to 87 families before the shipment was flagged. Formaldehyde is a preservative used in some international fish processing — illegal at these concentrations.
Compound: CH₂O
Case 2 — Vanilla Extract Batch VE-203
Methanol in Place of Ethanol
Bootleg vanilla extract substituted methanol for ethanol. Three children hospitalized with vision loss. Methanol and ethanol have nearly identical structures — the one-carbon difference makes methanol lethal.
Compound: CH₃OH
Case 3 — Kitchen Equipment Malfunction
Carbon Monoxide Buildup
Faulty stoves in the community kitchen serving 200 meals per day. CO is odorless and colorless, making it undetectable without equipment. It binds hemoglobin and blocks oxygen transport at the cellular level.
Compound: CO
Case 4 — Decomposing Food Storage
Hydrogen Sulfide from Power Outage
500 lbs of protein-rich food spoiled during a power outage. H₂S released during decomposition, creating an immediate evacuation hazard.
Compound: H₂S

Section V

Learning Objectives

By the end of Week 4, you will be able to:

  1. Draw accurate Lewis structures for simple molecules including bonding and lone pairs
  2. Use VSEPR theory to predict 3D molecular geometry from a Lewis structure
  3. Identify molecular shapes: linear, bent, trigonal planar, tetrahedral, trigonal pyramidal
  4. Predict molecular polarity based on geometry and bond polarity
  5. Apply “like dissolves like” to predict solubility in water versus oil
  6. Explain how molecular shape affects biological function and toxicity
  7. Connect molecular polarity to tissue accumulation patterns in the body
  8. Use molecular properties to make evidence-based food safety decisions
Section VI

Common Questions

Do I need special materials for the molecular model building?
No — marshmallows and toothpicks work well and are intentionally low-cost. If you prefer software, PyMOL is a free molecular visualization tool. Both approaches satisfy the lab requirements. Check the Lab 3 instructions for specific guidance on whichever method you choose.
How do I choose which molecules to analyze?
The lab instructions specify the molecule sets for each part. You choose a subset from within each set — pick ones you find most interesting or that connect most directly to the Bracket City cases above. There is no wrong choice as long as you follow the selection criteria in the instructions.
I have both Lab 2 and Lab 3 work this week — how do I manage the workload?
Lab 2 should already be mostly complete from last week — this week you are writing the report around data you already collected. Prioritize finishing that early in the week (Monday or Tuesday) so the rest of the week is focused entirely on Lab 3. The timeline above is structured with this in mind.
When is the full Lab 3 report due?
The data tables and model documentation are due at the end of Week 4. The full written report is due at the end of Week 5, giving you time to write a quality analysis after completing the hands-on work.
