Situation Report

What Is Happening in Bracket City?

Bracket City is a community facing a series of interconnected food safety emergencies. The city's food bank serves over 5,000 residents, including children, seniors, and families who depend on it for nutritional support. Over the next 7.5 weeks, you will serve as the investigating chemist assigned to this case.

This is not a memorization course. There are no traditional exams. Every assignment you complete produces a real product—a safety recommendation, a contamination report, a community recipe—that matters to Bracket City's residents. The chemistry you engage with in each chapter provides the analytical tools you need to investigate each crisis.

Bracket City — Mission Briefing
Your Role: Food Safety Chemist

You have been assigned to Bracket City's Department of Health. Multiple food safety crises have emerged simultaneously, and you are part of the investigative team. Each mission builds on the last. The evidence you collect in Mission 1 informs Mission 2. Your recommendations accumulate into a final case file presented at the end of the course.

Real-World Context — Why This Matters

Food insecurity affects 1 in 7 Americans, and food banks are often the last line of defense for vulnerable populations. Food fraud, heavy metal contamination, agricultural runoff, and inadequate water quality are documented, ongoing public health problems—not hypothetical scenarios invented for a classroom.

The chemistry concepts in this course are the same tools used by actual food safety regulators, public health chemists, and environmental scientists. You will use them the same way: to collect data, analyze evidence, and make recommendations that protect people.

The Investigation

Five Missions. Five Crises. One Course.

Each lab is a chapter in the same ongoing investigation. Missions are sequenced intentionally—concepts from earlier cases appear in later ones. Engage with the chapter content before each lab. The chemistry is your investigative toolkit.

01
Food Fraud Scandal
Multiple liquid food shipments have arrived mislabeled or potentially adulterated. Watered-down milk and fake honey have entered the supply chain. You will detect adulteration using density as a physical fingerprint unique to each pure substance.
Chapter 1 — Density, Matter Classification & Physical Properties
02
Heavy Metal Contamination
Toxic metals have been detected in incoming food shipments. You must use the periodic table to distinguish essential nutrient elements from dangerous contaminants, and evaluate whether fortified or whole foods better protect Bracket City's most vulnerable residents.
Chapter 2 — Periodic Table, Elements & Nutritional Chemistry
03
Agricultural Chemical Scare
Runoff from nearby farms may have introduced chemical compounds into the food supply. Molecular structure and polarity determine how these compounds behave in biological systems and whether they pose a health risk at detected concentrations.
Chapter 3 — Molecular Structure, Bonding & Polarity
04
Water Treatment Challenge
The food bank is expanding its meal program to serve 5,000 residents. Water quality must be verified and treatment solutions evaluated before the expanded program can launch safely.
Chapter 4 — Solutions, Concentration & Water Quality
05
Community Kitchen Crisis
Families receiving food bank support have no ovens and limited stoves. You will develop safe, nutritious no-cook recipes for the Bracket City Community Cookbook, using AI as an investigative tool—and critically evaluating its output against human-tested recipes.
Chapter 5 — Solubility, Polarity, Electrolytes & AI Evaluation
Course Design

How This Course Works

No traditional exams. No passive homework sets. Everything you produce connects directly to Bracket City. Expand each section to understand how the components work together.

01
Chapter Content — Your Investigative Toolkit
+

Each chapter in Canvas contains the chemistry background required to complete the associated mission. This content is not supplementary reading—it is the analytical foundation for your lab work and workbook problems.

The sequence within each module is intentional: Chapter content first → Lab investigation → Workbook application. Students who engage with chapter content before the lab find the analysis questions significantly more tractable and produce stronger conclusions.

If you find yourself stuck on an analysis question in a lab, return to the chapter. The conceptual answer is there.

02
Labs (Missions 1–5) — Hands-On Investigation
+

All five labs are conducted in your home or dorm room using household materials: a food scale, kitchen liquids, marshmallows and toothpicks, pH strips, and pantry ingredients. No campus lab time required.

Every lab submission requires completed data tables, written analysis questions, a written conclusion, and photographs documenting your setup and results. Photographs are not optional—they verify that you conducted the investigation.

Handwritten calculations are required for all quantitative work. Typed calculations receive half credit. Units must appear in every numerical answer; missing units result in point deductions.

03
Integrated Workbooks — Concept Application
+

Workbooks are hosted in Canvas alongside chapter content and completed after the relevant labs. They replace traditional exams by requiring you to apply chemistry concepts to new Bracket City scenarios.

Workbook A (Chapters 1–2): Density, matter classification, the periodic table, and element identification as applied to food safety scenarios from Missions 1 and 2.

Workbook B (Chapters 3–4): Molecular structure, polarity, solutions, and concentration as applied to the contamination and water quality cases in Missions 3 and 4.

Each workbook uses a choice-based format. You select which problem sets to complete to reach the required point total, directing your own focus within the content.

04
AI Integration — Tool, Not Substitute
+

This course treats AI as a professional tool requiring critical judgment. Knowing when and how to use AI responsibly is an increasingly important skill in any scientific or technical career.

Allowed Lab 5 recipe generation, research brainstorming, data organization (cite tool used)

Restricted All calculations, data analysis, lab conclusions, workbook problem sets

Required Lab 2 discussion board synthesis — must cite the AI tool used

In Lab 5 you will directly compare an AI-generated recipe to a human-tested recipe, evaluating which is safer, more accurate, and better suited to Bracket City's constraints. This is applied critical evaluation, not policy compliance.

05
Collaboration — Real Scientists Work Together
+

Finding a lab partner is encouraged. You can share materials, collect data together, and discuss findings. Sharing a food scale cuts costs. Two sets of measurements improve data quality.

Each student submits their own individual lab report and workbook. Your written analysis, conclusions, and calculations must represent your own thinking. Copied or fully AI-generated submissions without proper disclosure receive a zero.

Discussion boards in Labs 2 and 5 are collaborative by design. You will need your classmates' data to complete the full synthesis—no individual can analyze all 52 food bank inventory items alone. This mirrors how professional food safety investigations actually function.

Submission Policies
What Affects Your Grade

Handwritten calculations are required; typed calculations receive 50% credit with resubmission allowed. Missing units on any numerical answer result in a 0.5 pt deduction per problem. Photographs are required in every lab and cannot be retroactively awarded. Copied or undisclosed AI-generated submissions receive a zero.
