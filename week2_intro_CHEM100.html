Section V

Key Concepts to Master

Concept 01
Classification Hierarchy
Pure substances (elements and compounds) versus mixtures (homogeneous and heterogeneous). Every food item you test this week fits somewhere in this hierarchy.
Salt = compound  •  Salt water = mixture  •  Sodium metal = element
Concept 02
Density as an Intensive Property
Density does not depend on the amount of substance — this is exactly what makes it useful for identification. A small sample gives you the same fingerprint as a large one.
1 drop of honey and 1 gallon of honey have the same density
Concept 03
Density = Mass / Volume
ρ = m/v    Units: g/mL or g/cm³ (equivalent). Always include units. Missing units = missing meaning.
If honey’s density is too low, it may be diluted with corn syrup
Section VI

Why This Matters

Real-World Context

In 2008, criminals in China diluted milk with water and added melamine to falsely inflate protein readings. Six infants died and 300,000 children were sickened. Density testing could have flagged the adulteration before distribution. You are learning the same analytical tools used by food safety regulators to protect vulnerable populations.

Section VII

Common Questions

My water density is not exactly 1.0 g/mL. Did I make an error?
Probably not. Temperature affects density, and small measurement errors are normal in any lab setting. A result within 0.05 g/mL of the expected value is acceptable. Document what you observed and move on.
Can I test the same liquids as other students?
Yes. Water, oil, and milk are required for all students. From there, choose 2 or more additional liquids. Variety is encouraged but not required — consistency in your measurement technique matters more than which liquids you pick.
Does the discussion have to be a written post?
No. The discussion offers a video response option if you prefer to speak your answer. Either format is fully accepted — choose whichever allows you to communicate most clearly.
When exactly is the Lab 1 report due if the data table is due this Sunday?
The full written report is due the Sunday of Week 3. This week you submit your data table to confirm the experiment is complete. The report gives you time to write a quality analysis after reviewing your results.
